At everyday scales, objects behave predictably: a ball has a definite position and speed, and we can track it smoothly. In the quantum world of electrons and photons, things are different: particles can behave like waves, spreading out and interfering, so we can only talk about probabilities of where they might be found. When we say an electron is in “superposition”, we mean it is not in one exact place or state until we measure it; the measurement forces one outcome and destroys the others, like a coin that is spinning in the air and only shows heads or tails when it lands. Quantum mechanics is the set of rules that describe these strange but precise behaviors, and despite sounding weird, it predicts experiments with incredible accuracy.
